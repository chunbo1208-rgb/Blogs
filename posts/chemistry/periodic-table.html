The Periodic Table: Order in the Elements

Chunbo Liu Inorganic Chemistry 2026-03-31

The periodic table organizes all known chemical elements by atomic number, electron configuration, and recurring chemical properties. We explore the logic behind its structure — periods, groups, and blocks — and why Mendeleev's original insight still holds at the frontier of element synthesis.

Why a Table?

By 1869, 63 elements were known. Dmitri Mendeleev noticed that when elements were arranged by increasing atomic mass, properties repeated at regular intervals — a periodicity. He arranged them in rows (periods) such that elements with similar chemical behavior fell in the same column (group).

More boldly, he left gaps where no known element fit, predicting their properties from the surrounding elements. The subsequent discovery of gallium (1875), scandium (1879), and germanium (1886) — each matching his predictions closely — was a triumph of the table's predictive power.

ℹ The modern table orders elements by atomic number (protons), not atomic mass. This resolved anomalies like tellurium (Z=52) appearing before iodine (Z=53) despite having greater mass.

Structure of the Table

Periods and Electron Shells

Each horizontal row is a period. Moving across a period, one proton and one electron are added at a time. The period number corresponds to the highest principal quantum number $n$ of electrons in that row's ground-state atoms. Period 2 (Li → Ne) fills the $2s$ and $2p$ subshells:

$$\text{Li: } 1s^2\,2s^1 \quad \longrightarrow \quad \text{Ne: } 1s^2\,2s^2\,2p^6$$

Groups and Valence Electrons

Each vertical column is a group. Elements in the same group share the same number of valence electrons and therefore similar chemical behavior. Group 1 (alkali metals) all have one valence electron and react vigorously with water:

$$2\,\text{Na} + 2\,\text{H}_2\text{O} \;\longrightarrow\; 2\,\text{NaOH} + \text{H}_2\uparrow$$

Periodic Trends

Because nuclear charge increases left-to-right across a period while electrons fill the same shell, several properties change predictably:

⚡ Atomic radius decreases across a period (more protons pull electrons closer) and increases down a group (new shells are farther from the nucleus).
⚡ Ionization energy — the energy to remove an electron — generally increases across a period and decreases down a group, inversely tracking atomic radius.
⚡ Electronegativity (Pauling scale) peaks at fluorine ($\chi = 3.98$), the most electronegative element. It increases across periods and decreases down groups.

The Frontier: Superheavy Elements

Element 118, oganesson (Og), currently sits at the bottom-right of the table. Synthesized in 2002 by bombarding californium-249 with calcium-48 ions, only a handful of atoms have ever been produced. Its half-life is roughly 0.7 ms.

$${}^{249}_{98}\text{Cf} + {}^{48}_{20}\text{Ca} \;\longrightarrow\; {}^{294}_{118}\text{Og} + 3\,n$$

Relativistic effects — arising because inner electrons near such a heavy nucleus travel at a significant fraction of $c$ — distort the chemical behavior of superheavy elements in ways that challenge our group-based predictions. The table's elegant order meets its limits here, yet those limits are precisely what makes the frontier so interesting.

⚠ Relativistic contraction of the 6s orbital in gold gives it its distinctive yellow color and exceptional corrosion resistance — properties that a non-relativistic calculation would entirely miss.
