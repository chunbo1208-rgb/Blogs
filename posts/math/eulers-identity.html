Euler's Identity: The Most Beautiful Equation

Chunbo Liu Analysis Complex Numbers 2026-03-29

Of all the equations in mathematics, one stands above the rest — not for its computational utility, but for its sheer audacity in uniting the five most fundamental constants in a single, breathtaking statement.

$$e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0$$

This is Euler's identity. It contains: the base of natural logarithms $e \approx 2.71828$, the imaginary unit $i = \sqrt{-1}$, the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter $\pi \approx 3.14159$, the multiplicative identity $1$, and the additive identity $0$.

Euler's Formula

The identity is a special case of the more general Euler's formula, which relates complex exponentials to trigonometric functions:

$$e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$$

Substituting $\theta = \pi$ and using $\cos\pi = -1$, $\sin\pi = 0$:

$$e^{i\pi} = \cos\pi + i\sin\pi = -1 + 0 \cdot i = -1$$

Hence $e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0$. The proof is almost trivial once you have the formula — the real work is establishing the formula itself.

Proof via Taylor Series

Define the exponential function by its Taylor series — valid everywhere in $\mathbb{C}$:

$$e^z = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{z^n}{n!} = 1 + z + \frac{z^2}{2!} + \frac{z^3}{3!} + \cdots$$

Substituting $z = i\theta$, and noting the pattern of powers of $i$:

Powers of $i$ cycle with period 4: $\;i^0=1,\; i^1=i,\; i^2=-1,\; i^3=-i,\; i^4=1, \ldots$

Separating real and imaginary parts:

$$e^{i\theta} = \underbrace{\left(1 - \frac{\theta^2}{2!} + \frac{\theta^4}{4!} - \cdots\right)}_{\cos\theta} + i\underbrace{\left(\theta - \frac{\theta^3}{3!} + \frac{\theta^5}{5!} - \cdots\right)}_{\sin\theta}$$
For all $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$: $$e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$$

The Taylor series for $e^z$ converges absolutely for all $z \in \mathbb{C}$ (by the ratio test: $|z|^{n+1}/(n+1)! \to 0$). Substituting $z = i\theta$ and grouping even and odd powers:

$$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(i\theta)^n}{n!} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^k\theta^{2k}}{(2k)!} + i\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^k\theta^{2k+1}}{(2k+1)!} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta.$$

Geometric Interpretation

On the complex plane, multiplying by $e^{i\theta}$ is a rotation by angle $\theta$ about the origin. The unit circle — all complex numbers $z$ with $|z|=1$ — can be parametrized exactly as:

$$z = e^{i\theta}, \quad \theta \in [0, 2\pi)$$

Euler's identity says: start at $1$ on the real axis, rotate by $\pi$ radians (half a turn), and you land at $-1$. One step further ($2\pi$) returns you home. The whole geometry of the circle is encoded in a single exponential.

Why It Matters

Beyond aesthetics, Euler's formula is foundational to Fourier analysis, quantum mechanics (the Schrödinger equation uses $e^{i\omega t}$ extensively), electrical engineering (phasors), and signal processing. The identity is not a curiosity — it is the reason complex analysis is so unreasonably effective.
