Mitosis: The Dance of Division

Chunbo Liu Cell Biology 2026-03-27

Mitosis is the process by which a eukaryotic cell divides its duplicated genome equally into two daughter cells. We walk through the five canonical phases — prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase, and cytokinesis — and discuss the regulatory machinery that makes the process so precise.

Why Division Must Be Precise

A human body contains approximately $3.7 \times 10^{13}$ cells, each carrying a nearly identical copy of the genome. During your lifetime, an estimated $10^{16}$ cell divisions occur. At this scale, even an error rate of $10^{-9}$ per division per base pair would produce millions of mutations.

Mitosis is the cell's solution to this problem: a highly choreographed mechanical process that physically separates sister chromatids with extraordinary fidelity.

ℹ Mitosis (M phase) is distinct from meiosis. Mitosis produces two genetically identical daughter cells; meiosis produces four genetically unique gametes with half the chromosome count.

The Five Phases

1. Prophase

Chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes, each consisting of two sister chromatids held together at the centromere. The mitotic spindle begins to form from the centrosomes, which migrate to opposite poles of the cell. The nuclear envelope starts to break down.

2. Metaphase

Chromosomes align at the metaphase plate — an imaginary plane equidistant between the two poles. Each chromosome is attached to spindle microtubules from both poles via kinetochore proteins. The spindle assembly checkpoint (SAC) holds the cell here until every chromosome is properly bioriented.

⚡ Spindle Assembly Checkpoint: Mad2 and BubR1 proteins monitor unattached kinetochores. A single unattached kinetochore is sufficient to halt progression to anaphase.

3. Anaphase

Once the SAC is satisfied, separase cleaves cohesin — the protein complex holding sister chromatids together. The chromatids are pulled to opposite poles by shortening kinetochore microtubules (kinesin and dynein motors) and by elongation of the spindle midzone (anaphase B).

4. Telophase

Chromosomes arrive at the poles and begin to decondense. Nuclear envelopes reassemble around each set of chromosomes, and the spindle disassembles.

5. Cytokinesis

The cytoplasm divides via a contractile ring of actin and myosin II that constricts at the cell equator, pinching off two daughter cells. In plant cells, a cell plate forms instead (no contractile ring).

The Numbers

A typical human somatic cell division:

PhaseDurationKey Event
G₁ (interphase)~11 hoursGrowth, preparation
S phase~8 hoursDNA replication
G₂~4 hoursCheckpoint, further growth
Mitosis (M)~1 hourChromosome segregation
Cytokinesis~30 minCell splitting

The entire cell cycle thus spans roughly 24 hours for a typical proliferating human cell — with mitosis itself occupying only about 4% of that time.

⚠ Cancer cells often disable checkpoint mechanisms (p53, Rb, SAC), allowing division to proceed despite DNA damage or chromosome misattachment.
