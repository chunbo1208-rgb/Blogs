DNA Replication: Reading the Blueprint

Chunbo Liu Molecular Biology 2026-03-26

Every time a cell divides, it must copy roughly 3 billion base pairs of DNA with extraordinary fidelity. This post covers the core machinery of eukaryotic DNA replication: helicase, primase, DNA polymerase, and the asymmetry between the leading and lagging strands.

The Core Problem

DNA is a double helix — two antiparallel strands wound around each other. To copy it, you must first unwind it. But DNA polymerase can only synthesize in the $5' \to 3'$ direction, and the two template strands run in opposite directions. This creates a fundamental asymmetry that the cell solves with elegant machinery.

ℹ DNA polymerase cannot start a new strand from scratch — it can only extend an existing 3'-OH end. This is why primase must first lay down a short RNA primer before polymerase can begin.

Leading vs. Lagging Strand

Because polymerase only runs $5' \to 3'$, the two template strands are copied very differently:

The leading strand is synthesized continuously — the polymerase follows the fork as it opens, producing one uninterrupted new strand.

The lagging strand must be synthesized in short segments — called Okazaki fragments (~150–200 bp in eukaryotes) — each requiring its own primer. These fragments are later joined by DNA ligase after the primers are replaced with DNA.

⚡ Okazaki fragments were discovered by Reiji and Tsuneko Okazaki in the 1960s using pulse-chase experiments with radioactive thymidine. The observation of short labeled fragments confirmed the discontinuous nature of lagging strand synthesis.

Fidelity: How Accurate Is It?

The raw error rate of DNA polymerase is approximately $10^{-5}$ per base pair. With proofreading (3'→5' exonuclease activity), this drops to $\sim 10^{-7}$. After mismatch repair, the final error rate is:

$$\epsilon \approx 10^{-9} \text{ errors per base pair per replication}$$

Across a $3 \times 10^9$ bp genome, this means roughly 3 mutations per cell division — a remarkable achievement given the speed (the human replication fork moves at $\sim 1{,}000$ bp/s).

⚠ Defects in mismatch repair genes (MLH1, MSH2, MSH6) cause Lynch Syndrome, dramatically increasing lifetime risk of colorectal and other cancers due to accumulated replication errors.

The End Replication Problem

Linear chromosomes face a fundamental problem: each round of replication shortens the lagging strand template at the 5' end (because the terminal RNA primer is removed but cannot be replaced). Without a solution, chromosomes would shrink with every division.

The solution is telomerase — a reverse transcriptase that carries its own RNA template and extends chromosome ends by adding TTAGGG repeats. Telomerase is active in stem cells and is re-activated in ~85% of cancers, granting cellular immortality.
