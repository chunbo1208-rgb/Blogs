On Reading Slowly

Chunbo Liu Reflection 2026-03-27

I read about forty pages an hour when I'm moving fast. At that pace, I can get through a 300-page book in a weekend. I almost never do this, because I've learned that speed and understanding are inversely related — at least for the books worth reading.

The books that have mattered most to me were the ones I read at roughly the pace of thought: stopping when something was unclear, re-reading sentences that felt important, sitting with a paragraph for ten minutes before continuing. Twenty pages an hour, maybe. Sometimes five.

"To read slowly is to give a book the same respect you would give a conversation."

The Illusion of Coverage

There is a kind of reading that optimizes for coverage — finishing books, marking them off a list, being able to say "I've read that." This is understandable. It's also largely useless. You do not absorb a book by completing it. You absorb it by living with it long enough for it to change something in how you think.

Speed-reading is a technology for people who have forgotten why they started reading.

What Slow Reading Actually Looks Like

It looks like a lot of stopping. It looks like reading the same page twice. It looks like writing in the margins — not summaries, but reactions: "This is wrong because —", "I've never considered this before", "This contradicts what I believed yesterday."

It looks like setting a book down for three days because one chapter gave you enough to think about. It looks like reading one difficult book alongside three easier ones, using the easier ones to rest.

The Books That Resist You

The best books are the ones that don't give themselves up easily. They require effort, and the effort is not a bug — it is the mechanism by which the book does its work. A book you can read in one sitting without friction has probably not changed anything in you. The friction is the point.

Wittgenstein's Tractatus, Simone Weil's Gravity and Grace, anything by Borges — these reward rereading in a way that airport novels do not. Not because they are obscure for its own sake, but because they are genuinely dense with thought, and thought takes time to unpack.

"Some books are to be tasted, others to be swallowed, and some few to be chewed and digested." — Francis Bacon

Most of what I read is to be tasted. A small fraction is worth digesting. The mistake is applying the same speed to both.
